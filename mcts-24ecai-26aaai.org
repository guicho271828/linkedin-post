Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) revolutionized board games.
AlphaGo, AlphaZero -- the results speak for themselves.
So when people tried to apply MCTS to classical planning, the natural instinct was:
just use the same algorithm. UCB1 worked for Go, it should work for planning, right?

Wrong. And this is a cautionary tale about blindly importing ML techniques into new domains without understanding it.

Here's the issue.
UCB1 assumes rewards are bounded in a known, fixed range [0, c].
In Go, this is fine: outcomes are +1/-1 (win/loss).
But in classical planning, the "rewards" are heuristic cost-to-go estimates.
These are non-negative, unbounded, vary wildly across different parts of the search tree,
and their range is unknown a priori. Every single assumption of UCB1 is violated.
Does the underlying bandit theory even apply here?

In ECAI 2024, we showed otherwise.
MCTS never consistently outperformed even the simplest baseline -- Greedy Best-First Search (GBFS).
To fix this,
we proposed UCB1-Normal2, a Gaussian bandit that adapts its exploration rate to the estimated variance of each subtree.
The result was the first MCTS variant to consistently outperform GBFS across diverse planning domains.

But we weren't done. Two theoretical gaps remained:

1. The Gaussian distribution has support (-∞, +∞), but costs are non-negative. An under-specification.
2. Why does Full Bellman backup (taking the minimum) work?
   People keep saying, "taking the average (Monte Carlo backup) is 'rather odd' for planning", without justification.

In AAAI 2026, we resolved both issues with Extreme Value Theory (EVT)
-- the statistical framework for modeling extrema rather than averages.
While most of ML relies on the Central Limit Theorem (averages converge to Gaussians),
planning is fundamentally about finding the minimum cost: statistics of extremes.

We showed that the tail behavior of cost-to-go follows a Generalized Pareto distribution.
This directly justifies Full Bellman backup:
the GP's upper support parameter estimates the minimum cost in a subtree,
and values below the threshold are excluded -- including dead-ends, which naturally fall outside the tail model.

The result, GUCT-Uniform, outperformed GBFS by 67.8 instances, GUCT-Normal2 by 23.4,
and Max-k bandits by over 300 instances.

The broader lesson: When you apply an algorithm from one domain to another, you must ask:
- What distributional assumptions does it make?
- Do they hold in my new setting?
- What is the right assumption for my problem?

In planning, the right model isn't averages (CLT/Gaussian) -- it's extremes (EVT/Generalized Pareto).
Getting this right turned a decade of underwhelming MCTS results into state-of-the-art performance.

Don't just import techniques. Import understanding.

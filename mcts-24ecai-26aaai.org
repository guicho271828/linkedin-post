Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) revolutionized board games.
AlphaGo, AlphaZero -- the results speak for themselves.
So when people tried to apply MCTS to automated planning, the natural instinct was:
just use the same algorithm. UCB1 worked for Go, it should work for planning, right?

Wrong. And this is a cautionary tale about blindly importing ML techniques into new domains without understanding the theory.

Here's the issue.
UCB1 -- the bandit algorithm at the heart of UCT/MCTS -- assumes rewards are bounded in a known, fixed range [0, c] shared across all arms.
In Go, this is fine: outcomes are +1/-1 (win/loss).
But in classical planning, the "rewards" are heuristic cost-to-go estimates.
These are non-negative, unbounded, vary wildly across different parts of the search tree,
and their range is unknown a priori. Every single assumption of UCB1 is violated.

The result? MCTS for planning never consistently outperformed even the simplest baseline -- Greedy Best-First Search (GBFS).
For over a decade, people tweaked implementations without asking:
does the underlying bandit theory even apply here?

In our ECAI 2024 paper, we showed that it doesn't.
We proposed UCB1-Normal2, a Gaussian bandit that adapts its exploration rate to the estimated variance of each subtree.
The resulting algorithm was the first MCTS variant to consistently outperform GBFS across diverse planning domains.

But we weren't done. Two theoretical gaps remained:

1. The Gaussian distribution has support (-inf, +inf), but heuristic values are non-negative. This is still an over-specification.
2. Why does Full Bellman backup (taking the minimum) work?
   The original MCTS papers used averaging (Monte Carlo backup) and just noted that the minimum was "rather odd" for planning -- without bandit-theoretic justification.

In our AAAI 2026 paper, we resolved both issues by introducing Extreme Value Theory (EVT)
-- the statistical framework for modeling extrema rather than averages.
While most of ML relies on the Central Limit Theorem (averages converge to Gaussians),
planning is fundamentally about finding the minimum cost path. We need statistics of extremes.

Using Peaks-Over-Threshold EVT,
we showed that the tail behavior of heuristic values follows a Generalized Pareto distribution with a finite upper bound (short-tailed).
This directly justifies the use of Full Bellman backup:
the GP's upper support parameter estimates the minimum heuristic in a subtree,
and values below the threshold are excluded -- including dead-ends, which naturally fall outside the tail model.

The resulting algorithm, GUCT-Uniform, outperformed GBFS by 67.8 instances, GUCT-Normal2 by 23.4,
the state-of-the-art diversified search Softmin-Type(h) by 33.2, and Max-k bandits (a similarly-named but fundamentally different framework) by over 300 instances.

The broader lesson: statistical thinking matters. When you apply an algorithm from one domain to another, you must ask:
- What distributional assumptions does this method make?
- Do they hold in my new setting?
- What is the right model for my problem?

In planning, the right model isn't about averages (CLT/Gaussian) -- it's about extremes (EVT/Generalized Pareto).
Getting this right turned a decade of underwhelming MCTS results into consistent state-of-the-art performance.

Don't just import techniques. Import understanding.

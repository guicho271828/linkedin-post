Imagine millions of customer complaints; an industry-scale workload.
Your task: Figure out which one to handled first,
or what does a typical complaint look like,
or how many groups of complaints exist.

How do you solve them with LLMs?
Just dump them all in one big context and hope for the best?
It fails because long context models "rots" [1].
We tested ourselves: Find the max over N=10^3 random integers.
(Imagine each number was a customer complaint instead.)
In random simulations,
the numbers returned by Qwen3-32B ranked ~42nd/1000th,
taking ~100sec per list --- mediocre for such a simple task.
Think tags did nothing; x10 slower yet the accuracy stays.
With N=10^4, it exceeded the 1hr time limit due to Transformer's O(N^2) runtime.
The "dump everything" strategy clearly does not scale.

Curiously, when N=100, it was much more accurate: Answers ranked 1.42-th on average.
What it shows is that *LLMs have no algorithmic thinking*.
If it did,
it would split N=10^3 list into N=100 sublists, find max of each, then find the global max.
With recursions and parallelism, this tournament-based maximum is O(log N).
*Reasoning is a fundamentally algorithmic task with task decomposition.*
It enables runtime analysis which is impossible in free-form generation.

This led us to ask: what if we use LLMs inside algorithms, not as general reasoners?
We call this paradigm Verbalized Algorithm (VA): Classical algorithms remastered for LLMs.
We keep their control flow (for/if/recursion) intact,
but replace their atomic operations with an LLM oracle that answers simple, single-step query: yes-no, or multiple-choice questions.
VAs give LLMs runtime guarantees;
O(log n) parallel tournament processed 10000 numbers in ~56sec with ALMOST NO mistakes; Answers ranked ~1.29th/10000th.

The big picture:
VA answers a key question "How to make LLMs reason reliably?"
Two other approaches also tackle it but
they are neither scalable or reliable due to the lack of sound complexity analysis.

Formalization translates a task into a symbolic form, solve it with a solver, then explain the output for users.
One of its key weaknesses is the expressivity of the solver's input DSL.
Modeling all the subtlety of the natural language in a symbolic DSL is impossible!

LLM-modulo-theory (by great Prof. Subbarao Kambhampati)
uses LLMs as a sampling distribution
in a verifier-based rejection sampler or a feedback-based sequential Monte-Carlo.
Can a (symbolic) verifier verify natural language?
Will the loop stop, when (= runtime)?
Is the solution practical (= optimal)?

Other work have embedded LLMs in algorithms [2,3],
yet we believe ours is the first general reductionist proposal toward LLM reasoning.

Traditional computer science all employ task decomposition to scale.
A merge sort splits the list in half, sort them individually, and combines the results.
At the lowest level, it reduces to a simple binary comparison.
These procedures, called algorithms, guarantee the correctness of their outputs and the average/worst-case runtime/memory.
In contrast, the opaqueness of LLM's reasoning prevents such an analysis.

We found the same phenomenon across a variety of tasks --- list sorting and submodular maximization.

*Pros*: This keeps the original complexity results (time/memory), parallelism, or probabilistic error rate analysis.
It also offloads multi-step reasoning from LLMs to battle-tested algorithms, so the small language models are enough!

*Cons*: Many LLM calls, but they are all short queries on small models!
